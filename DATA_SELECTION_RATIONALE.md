### Data Selection Rationale: Why These Specific Indicators?

#### Dependent Variable: Equity Market Performance

The **S&P 500 (^GSPC)** serves as the primary dependent variable in this analysis, representing the aggregate performance of large-cap US equities and maintaining a high degree of correlation with broader macroeconomic conditions. As a high-frequency continuous numerical time-series with daily trading observations, this instrument provides granular temporal resolution necessary for reliable statistical inference. However, equity price levels themselves exhibit pronounced non-stationarity—a defining characteristic of I(1) integrated processes—rendering them unsuitable for conventional regression-based hypothesis testing without mathematical transformation. Consequently, we compute continuously compounded percentage returns using the logarithmic transformation: r(t) = log(P(t)) - log(P(t-1)). This methodological step achieves stationarity in the observable data, mitigates heteroskedasticity in residuals, and facilitates the application of parametric statistical tests such as Welch's t-test under standard distributional assumptions.

#### Primary Independent Variables: Yield Curve Signals

The **10-Year US Treasury Yield (DGS10)** and **2-Year US Treasury Yield (DGS2)** constitute the core independent variables used to operationalize yield curve dynamics. These instruments represent the term structure of interest rates at two critical junctures of the maturity spectrum and are obtained at daily frequency from the Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) system. Their primary function is to calculate the Yield Curve Spread (DGS10 - DGS2), a continuous measure capturing the market's macroeconomic expectations. Following domain-specific conventions in financial econometrics, we subsequently engineer a categorical feature dichotomizing the yield curve into two regimes: 'Normal' (positive spread, DGS10 > DGS2) and 'Inverted' (negative spread, DGS10 ≤ DGS2). This categorical transformation enables hypothesis testing within a regime-contingent framework, allowing for the empirical evaluation of the "Late-Cycle Market Surge" hypothesis—specifically, whether mean S&P 500 returns differ significantly between normal monetary environment states and contractionary signal states.

#### Economic Regime Controls: Inflation Dynamics

The **Consumer Price Index (CPIAUCSL)** represents lower-frequency (monthly) continuous numerical data denoting aggregate consumer price dynamics. While inflation data is released at monthly periodicity rather than daily frequency, it provides essential context for segmenting the temporal analysis into distinct macroeconomic regimes differentiated by inflationary pressure intensity. We classify observations into 'High Inflation' and 'Low Inflation' regimes using empirically-derived median thresholds, enabling stratified analysis of equity market responses to yield curve signals under heterogeneous monetary policy environments. This segmentation accounts for the empirical observation that market reactions to term structure changes are conditioned upon existing inflationary state, thereby reducing specification bias and improving model robustness.

#### Macroeconomic Context: Lagging and Concurrent Indicators

The **Gross Domestic Product (GDP)** represents a low-frequency quarterly lagging indicator reflecting the realized state of economic activity with substantial reporting delays. Notwithstanding these temporal constraints, GDP serves as a fundamental verification mechanism for yield curve predictive content—inversions that precede economic contraction (negative GDP growth) validate the theoretical framework underlying our investigation. 

Conversely, the **US Dollar Index (DX-Y.NYB)** functions as a high-frequency daily concurrent indicator capturing real-time shifts in international currency valuations and capital flows. DXY exhibits significant co-movement with equity returns through multiple transmission channels: trade competitiveness effects, earnings repatriation considerations, and global capital reallocation dynamics. Its inclusion as a contextual control variable mitigates omitted variable bias stemming from contemporaneous macroeconomic shocks that may simultaneously affect both yield curve configuration and equity performance.

#### Methodological Implication: Frequency Alignment and Look-Ahead Bias

A critical methodological consideration emerges from the inherent heterogeneity in reporting frequencies across our dataset: Treasury yields (daily), CPI (monthly), GDP (quarterly), and equity prices (daily). To maintain temporal coherence without introducing look-ahead bias—a paramount concern in time-series finance—we employ forward-filling (ffill()) techniques to align all data to the daily trading calendar. Forward-filling preserves the information set available to market participants at each decision point by carrying the most recent observed value forward until a new observation becomes available, thereby honoring the temporal sequence of information revelation. This approach is methodologically superior to backward-filling or interpolation methods, which would violate causality constraints by implying access to future data. The resulting uniformly-sampled daily dataset enables valid application of Welch's t-test for regime-contingent analysis while maintaining strict adherence to data science best practices regarding temporal ordering and information asymmetry.
